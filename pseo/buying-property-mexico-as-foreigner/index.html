Buying Property in Mexico as a Foreigner: Complete Guide 2026

Poncho Davalos AMPI Associate, Riviera Nayarit
March 2026 — Updated: 2026-04-13

Your Complete Guide to Buying Property in Mexico as a Foreigner

Every week, I get calls and messages from Americans and Canadians who are excited about buying property in Mexico but overwhelmed by the process. They've heard about trusts, restricted zones, notaries, and closing costs, and it all sounds complicated. Here's the truth: it's simpler than you think, especially when you have the right guide by your side.

I'm Poncho Dávalos, an AMPI-certified real estate advisor based in Puerto Vallarta. Over the years, I've helped more than 100 foreign buyers navigate the Mexican real estate process from start to finish. In this guide, I'm going to walk you through everything you need to know — step by step, in plain English, with no legal jargon left unexplained.

Can Foreigners Own Property in Mexico? Yes — Here's How

Let's clear up the biggest misconception first: foreigners can absolutely own property in Mexico. This right is established in Mexico's constitution and supported by decades of legal precedent. However, there's one important distinction you need to understand.

The Restricted Zone

Mexico's constitution designates a "restricted zone" that includes all land within 50 kilometers (about 31 miles) of the coast and 100 kilometers (about 62 miles) of international borders. Puerto Vallarta, Cancún, Los Cabos, Playa del Carmen — all the places foreigners love most — fall within this zone.

Within the restricted zone, foreigners cannot hold direct title (escritura) to residential property. Instead, they use a mechanism called a fideicomiso, which is a bank trust. Don't let the word "trust" scare you — it's a well-established, government-regulated process that has been used successfully for decades.

The Fideicomiso Explained Simply

Think of the fideicomiso like this: a Mexican bank holds the legal title to your property in trust, and you are the beneficiary with full ownership rights. That means you can:

  • Live in the property or leave it vacant
  • Rent it out (short-term or long-term) and keep all the income
  • Remodel, expand, or make any improvements
  • Sell the property at any time and receive the proceeds
  • Pass the property to your heirs through your will
  • Use it as collateral for financing

The bank is simply the trustee — it has no claim on your property, cannot sell it without your authorization, and cannot benefit from it in any way. You are the owner in every practical sense.

Step-by-Step: The Buying Process

Step 1: Find Your Property and Make an Offer

Work with an AMPI-certified advisor to identify properties that match your goals — whether that's a vacation home, retirement residence, or investment property. Once you find the right one, we'll prepare a formal offer (oferta de compra) that outlines the price, terms, and conditions.

Step 2: Sign the Promise Agreement

Once the seller accepts your offer, both parties sign a promesa de compraventa (promise to buy/sell agreement). This is a binding contract that locks in the price and terms. You'll typically pay a deposit of 5-10% of the purchase price at this stage, held in escrow.

Step 3: Obtain Your RFC (Tax ID)

Every property buyer in Mexico needs an RFC (Registro Federal de Contribuyentes), which is essentially a Mexican tax ID number. This is required for the fideicomiso and for tax purposes. The process is straightforward and can often be completed by your advisor or notary on your behalf with a power of attorney.

Step 4: Apply for the Fideicomiso

Your advisor will help you select a bank and submit the fideicomiso application. The bank reviews the property documentation, verifies clear title, and obtains a permit from Mexico's Ministry of Foreign Affairs (SRE). This permit confirms that the government authorizes the trust for this specific property.

Costs: Fideicomiso setup typically runs $1,000 to $2,000 USD, with an annual maintenance fee of approximately $500 USD.

Step 5: Due Diligence and Title Search

The notary public conducts a thorough title search to verify:

  • Clear title with no liens or encumbrances
  • Property boundaries match official records
  • All taxes are current
  • No pending legal disputes
  • Proper zoning and land use permits

Step 6: Closing at the Notary

The closing takes place before a notary public (notario público), who is a government-appointed legal official — not to be confused with a notary public in the US or Canada. Mexican notaries are highly trained attorneys with special government authorization to certify legal transactions. The notary:

  • Reads the deed aloud to all parties
  • Verifies identities and legal capacity
  • Calculates and collects applicable taxes
  • Registers the transaction with the Public Registry of Property

Step 7: Registration and Possession

After closing, the notary registers the fideicomiso and the deed with the Public Registry. You receive your escritura (deed) and the keys to your new property. The entire process from accepted offer to keys in hand typically takes 4 to 8 weeks.

Understanding Closing Costs

Closing costs in Mexico generally range from 5% to 8% of the purchase price. Here's a typical breakdown:

  • Acquisition Tax (ISAI): 2-3% of assessed value
  • Notary Fees: 1-2% of transaction value
  • Fideicomiso Setup: $1,000-$2,000 USD
  • Certificate of No Liens: ~$100 USD
  • Public Registry Fee: ~$500 USD
  • Appraisal: ~$300-500 USD
  • SRE Permit: ~$200 USD

I always recommend budgeting 7% of the purchase price for closing costs to ensure you're comfortable and not caught off guard.

Power of Attorney: A Practical Tool

If you can't be physically present in Mexico for every step of the process, a power of attorney (poder notarial) allows a trusted representative — often your real estate advisor or attorney — to act on your behalf. This is a common and perfectly safe practice, especially for international buyers who may need to handle some steps remotely.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

In my experience helping over 100 foreign buyers, these are the mistakes I see most often:

  • Not verifying clear title: Always insist on a thorough title search through the notary. Never skip this step.
  • Working with uncertified agents: Choose an AMPI-certified advisor who is bound by professional ethics and continuing education requirements.
  • Forgetting about the RFC: You need a Mexican tax ID, and it's better to arrange this early in the process.
  • Underestimating closing costs: Budget 5-8% on top of your purchase price.
  • Rushing the process: Good due diligence takes time. A 4-8 week timeline is normal and healthy.
  • Not getting independent legal advice: While I guide my clients through every step, I also recommend having an independent attorney review major documents.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreigners legally buy property in Mexico?

Yes, foreigners can legally buy property anywhere in Mexico. In the restricted zone (within 50 km of the coast), a bank trust called fideicomiso is used, granting full ownership rights including the ability to sell, rent, remodel, and inherit.

How much does it cost to set up a fideicomiso in Mexico?

The cost of setting up a fideicomiso typically ranges from $1,000 to $2,000 USD, with an annual maintenance fee of approximately $500 USD. These costs are included in the total closing costs of 5-8% of the purchase price.

How long does it take to buy property in Mexico as a foreigner?

The typical process takes 4 to 8 weeks from accepted offer to closing. This includes obtaining permits, establishing the fideicomiso, notary public review, and property registration.

What are the most common mistakes foreigners make when buying in Mexico?

The most common mistakes include not verifying clear title, not working with an AMPI-certified advisor, failing to obtain an RFC (tax ID), and not budgeting for closing costs of 5-8%. An experienced advisor like Poncho Dávalos helps you avoid all of these pitfalls.
